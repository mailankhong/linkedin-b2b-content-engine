# Profile Optimizer

A LinkedIn profile isn't a resume — it's a landing page. In B2B it's also a positioning vehicle: the right buyer pre-qualifies you here 6-18 months before they're ready to buy. Most profiles describe what someone does. Optimized profiles make the right buyer think "this is the person for that problem." The difference is category clarity + authority proof + a credible next step — not the conversion-copy tricks borrowed from solopreneur playbooks.

This skill audits every section and rewrites it to convert the right B2B visitor — founder, operator, or agency owner — into followers, connections, warm inbounds, or demo bookings.

**Pre-flight:** Run preflight.md. Client: [name provided by user].
**Required:** Voice Profile, ICP, content pillars. Missing → stop.

## Quality Gate

Insufficient input → stop. State gap, impact, fix option. Never invent or fabricate.

---

## How It Works

1. **Audit** — collect profile content + confirm archetype (founder / operator / agency owner)
2. **Score & Diagnose** — 3-dimension rubric, cross-section alignment, ranked top 3 quick fixes
3. **Rewrite** — every section, in voice, B2B-appropriate

---

## Phase 1: Audit Input

Ask the user to provide:
- Headline
- About section (full text)
- Experience (at least current role + description)
- Featured section (what's pinned, if anything)
- Banner image description
- Profile photo description
- Any external link (website, calendar, company page)

If a screenshot is unreadable or sections are cut off → ask for the missing text. Don't guess.

**Also confirm the archetype — it changes everything downstream:**

> Quick question before I audit. Which best describes this profile?
> **A. Founder / CEO** — profile is a positioning vehicle for the company's category + offer
> **B. Operator / Exec / IC** — profile is personal authority / career capital, even if they work at a company
> **C. Agency owner / Consultant** — they sell their own service; company brand is secondary

The archetype controls:
- Headline framing (company category vs. personal expertise vs. service category)
- Proof sources (company metrics vs. personal track record vs. client outcomes)
- Conversion targets (demo/waitlist vs. newsletter/podcast vs. calendar/lead magnet)

Foundation context to pull (from client folder):
- ICP → who the profile must speak to
- Offer → what the profile must reinforce
- Content pillars → what themes recent posts will back up
- Voice Profile → language for rewrites

## Phase 2: Score & Diagnose

Score on **3 dimensions**, each /10. Use the rubrics below for consistent scoring across sessions and clients.

### Dimension 1: CLARITY

**Question:** Can a stranger understand who this person helps, what they do, and what outcome they deliver within 5 seconds?

**Evaluate:**
- **Headline** — names specific ICP (not "businesses" / "professionals"); names outcome or category; free of vague descriptors
- **About "above the fold"** — first 2-3 lines are all that's visible before "see more" on mobile. Offer must be clear in the first sentence; ideal buyer named
- **Banner** — reinforces or adds to the headline, not decorative noise
- **Consistency** — headline ↔ about ↔ featured ↔ experience all tell the same story; no outdated positioning

**Score bands:**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 9-10 | Crystal clear. A stranger identifies who they help, what they do, and why in under 5 seconds. Zero confusion. |
| 7-8 | Mostly clear. Core message present, but one element vague or one section contradicts. |
| 5-6 | Mixed signals. Some sections clear, others generic or stale. Reader has to piece it together. |
| 3-4 | Confusing. Multiple vague sections, inconsistent messaging, unclear ICP. |
| 1-2 | No clarity. Reads like a resume or LinkedIn default. |

### Dimension 2: AUTHORITY

**Question:** Does the profile establish that this person is the real deal — and for B2B specifically, does it establish **category ownership**?

**Evaluate:**
- **Social proof** — specific numbers (customers, revenue impact, deal sizes, years), named clients, recognizable logos, testimonials
- **Credibility signals** — professional photo, branded banner, relevant experience, featured section contains proof
- **Category ownership (B2B-critical)** — is there a named framework, category, or POV they're associated with? Would an insider say "oh, they're the [X] person"?
- **Role coherence** — ex-roles either add credibility (ex-Stripe, ex-McKinsey = keep) or dilute positioning (prune or demote)

**B2B-specific trust killers:**
- Default LinkedIn banner
- Personal-revenue claims that feel solopreneur-coded on a founder profile ("$X in client revenue generated") — use company/product metrics instead
- Old irrelevant side hustles that contradict current positioning (but don't prune credentialing roles just because they're "off-topic")
- Featured section empty or full of random reshared content

**Score bands:**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 9-10 | Strong authority + identifiable category ownership. Multiple proof points, credible background, named POV/framework. |
| 7-8 | Good authority. Proof present but missing one of: specific numbers, category ownership, or named framework. |
| 5-6 | Average. Experience is credible but no proof of results and no distinctive POV — reads like a competent bio. |
| 3-4 | Weak. Little proof, generic experience, no reason to trust over anyone else in the space. |
| 1-2 | No authority signals. Default visuals, resume-style content. |

### Dimension 3: CONVERSION POTENTIAL

**Question:** Is there a credible next step for the right B2B buyer — matched to where they are in the buying journey?

B2B CTAs are **not** "DM me KEYWORD." In B2B, the next step must match buyer temperature:

| Temperature | Appropriate next step |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Cold (just discovered you) | Follow, newsletter subscription, podcast, free whitepaper/report |
| Warm (recognizes you) | Product demo, case study download, waitlist, webinar |
| Hot (considering you) | Book a call, pilot request, contact sales |

**Evaluate:**
- Featured section: strategic (proof + category asset + next step) or random content?
- About section ends with a clear next step appropriate to archetype?
- Banner reinforces a next step or wastes the real estate?
- External link points somewhere useful (company site, podcast, newsletter) or missing/dead?
- Are there **multiple tiers** of next step for different buyer temperatures, or only "book a call" (too high friction for cold)?

**Score bands:**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 9-10 | Multiple tiers of next step, matched to buyer temperature. Featured is strategic. Clear path from curious → customer. |
| 7-8 | Good structure. Has next steps but missing one tier (e.g. no low-friction entry for cold visitors). |
| 5-6 | Partial. One CTA somewhere, but path is unclear or friction mismatched. |
| 3-4 | Weak. No low-friction entry, vague/missing CTAs, featured random. |
| 1-2 | No path at all. No external link, no CTA, no featured strategy. |

### Overall Score

Average the three dimensions. Include a one-sentence diagnosis.

Format:
```
CLARITY:              X/10
AUTHORITY:            X/10
CONVERSION POTENTIAL: X/10
─────────────────────────
OVERALL:              X/10

DIAGNOSIS: [one sentence — direct and honest]
```

### Cross-Section Misalignment Matrix

After scoring, run these 5 checks. Each misalignment = a specific quick-fix candidate.

| Check | Looking for |
|-------|-------------|
| Headline ↔ About | Same ICP, same outcome, same category? |
| Headline ↔ Banner | Banner reinforces or contradicts? |
| About ↔ Featured | Does featured back up the claims in about? |
| Experience ↔ current positioning | Old roles diluting, or adding credibility? |
| Profile ↔ recent content | Do the last 10-20 posts reinforce the headline's category? (B2B buyers check this — profile claims without matching content = distrust) |

### Top 3 Quick Fixes (ranked by impact)

Before rewriting everything, isolate the 3 highest-leverage changes. For each:

1. **What's wrong** (specific, not "your headline is weak")
2. **Why it matters** (connect to pipeline, trust, or category recognition)
3. **Exact rewrite or action** (copy-paste ready)

Rank by impact. Headline fixes usually go first — it's the only section that appears in search, feeds, and notifications. Featured-section strategy is usually #2 for profiles getting traffic but no conversion. About-section rewrite is #3 unless the current one is actively repelling people.

## Phase 3: Rewrite

### Headline — 5 options, each labeled with its angle

**What every headline must do:**
- Name the specific audience (not "businesses")
- Name the outcome, category, or transformation
- Be specific — numbers, named methods, concrete results where possible
- Match the archetype (founder → company category; operator → personal expertise; agency → service category)

**Banned vocabulary** (vague descriptors that kill specificity):
Consultant (alone), Coach, Strategist, Entrepreneur, Thought leader, Innovator, Passionate, Results-driven, "Helping businesses grow / scale," "Empowering [anyone]," "Driving growth."

**5 angles — deliver one headline per angle:**

**Angle 1: Outcome + specific audience**
"[Outcome] for [specific ICP] — [differentiator or constraint]"
Example (B2B SaaS founder): "Reliable browser automation for AI agent builders — without flaky sessions"

**Angle 2: Authority + specificity**
"[Credibility marker] | [What you do for whom]"
Example (founder): "Built the browser automation layer used by AI agent teams | Airtop CEO"
Example (operator): "Scaled [company] from $X to $Y ARR | Now leading [current role]"

**Angle 3: Category / method ownership**
"Creator of [named framework/category] | Helping [audience] [outcome]"
Example: "Architect of Enterprise Decision AI | Helping CPG leaders turn data into decisions"

**Angle 4: Pain-point lead (use carefully in B2B — must be credible, not gimmicky)**
"Most [audience] [common failure]. Here's what works instead."
Example: "Most AI agents break the moment they hit a real browser. Fixing that at Airtop."

**Angle 5: Role + company + category**
"[Role] at [Company] | [Category they own]"
Example: "Co-founder @ DataPoem | Enterprise Decision AI for CPG"

Label each delivered option with its angle so the client sees the strategic choice, not just wording.

### About Section — the "mini sales page"

Not a resume. Not a bio. A narrative.

**Lines 1-3: The hook** (all that's visible above "see more" on mobile)
- Name the problem or the category
- Name the ideal buyer
- Earn the "see more" click

Bad: "I'm a founder with 15 years of experience in enterprise software."
Good: "Most enterprise AI deployments stall because the data isn't decision-ready. We built DataPoem to fix that."

**Lines 4-7: The problem**
The pain the ICP is actually experiencing. Mirror their language (pull from ICP doc).

**Lines 8-12: What you do**
Your approach — briefly. Name the framework or category if you have one. Show the path from where they are to where they want to be.

**Lines 13-16: Proof**
Specific numbers, named clients, recognizable outcomes. No "many customers" or "significant impact" — use real numbers or flag `[INSERT REAL STAT]`.

**Lines 17-19: Next step (archetype-matched CTA)**
ONE clear action:
- **Founder** → product demo, newsletter, waitlist, or company site
- **Operator** → follow, newsletter, podcast, DM for a specific topic
- **Agency owner** → case studies, calendar, or a lead magnet (this is where solopreneur-style CTAs do work)

**Length:** 1,500-2,000 characters. Long enough to earn trust, short enough to actually read.

**Voice:** from the Voice Profile. First person, short paragraphs, no corporate speak.

### Featured Section — 3-slot strategic hierarchy (B2B-translated)

**Slot 1 — most compelling proof**
Named customer case study, product demo video, recognizable press/podcast, or the highest-performing post that embodies the category.
First thing a visitor sees. If they look at one thing, this is it.

**Slot 2 — category / credibility asset**
What reinforces category ownership: a flagship post, a framework/methodology explainer, a book/whitepaper, a speaking keynote video, a thought-leadership piece that defines their POV.
For agency owners and solo consultants: a lead magnet fits here.

**Slot 3 — strategic next step** (matched to archetype)
- Founder → product page, demo request, waitlist, investor page
- Operator → newsletter, podcast, personal site
- Agency owner → calendar, case studies page, application form

**Flag aggressively:** random reshares, outdated lead magnets for offers they no longer run, three slots all doing the same job (e.g. 3 similar posts), empty featured section.

### Experience Section

**Current role:**
Rewrite as a mini value prop, not a job description:
- Line 1: what the company does + for whom
- Line 2: the outcome it delivers (specific)
- Lines 3-5: proof points with numbers, named customers, or concrete traction

**Previous roles — the B2B dilution rule:**
Default: trim roles that dilute current positioning.
**Exception:** keep roles that carry **credibility collateral** even if off-topic (ex-Google, ex-Stripe, ex-McKinsey, ex-big-brand). In B2B, ex-company credibility frequently outweighs topical tightness.

Judgment-call test: "Does leaving this role here cost clarity more than it gains trust?"
- Yes → prune or compress.
- No → keep with a tightened description.

Random irrelevant side hustles → prune always.

### Banner

**Must communicate:**
1. A one-line value prop (simplified headline)
2. A credibility marker or social proof (named customer logos, specific number, category claim)
3. A directional next step (company URL, newsletter, demo CTA) — matched to archetype

**Design rules:**
- **Mobile readability is the gate.** If the text isn't legible on a phone, it doesn't exist. Test before delivering.
- High contrast
- One message — not five
- Clean and professional. No selfie banners for B2B. No default LinkedIn gradient.

**Common B2B banner mistakes to flag:**
- Default gradient = "doesn't take LinkedIn seriously"
- Pretty stock landscape with no text = wasted real estate
- Company logo only with no context = tells the viewer nothing
- Text legible only on desktop = invisible on mobile
- Cluttered = nothing is readable

### Profile Photo

- Professional, approachable, in focus
- Eye contact, confident, clear lighting
- Clean or intentional background
- Would a B2B buyer trust this person with a six- or seven-figure decision based on the photo alone?

## Edge Cases

- **Screenshot unreadable** → ask for text paste or better screenshot; don't guess.
- **No clear offer or positioning yet** → stop. A profile audit won't help until positioning is decided. Route to content-pillars or offer-positioning work first.
- **Profile is already strong** → don't manufacture issues. Score it high, give 2-3 refinements, redirect energy to content / DMs / demand gen.
- **Partial profile provided** → audit what you have, flag what's missing, offer to complete when they share the rest.
- **Founder profile where the company is the product** → lean heavily into company category language in headline and about. Treat the personal brand as the distribution layer for company positioning, not a standalone offer.

---

## Advanced Positioning Checklist — Final Gate

Before delivering, run these 6 checks. Any "no" = fix before shipping.

1. Does every section reinforce the archetype-appropriate offer/category?
2. Is social proof present in both About and Featured?
3. Have dilutive roles been pruned (or credibility roles retained with purpose)?
4. Are there **multiple tiers** of next step matched to buyer temperature?
5. Is the messaging tight across all sections — no outdated positioning, no contradictions?
6. Would the ICP, landing cold on this profile, add this person to their consideration set for their problem within 5 seconds?

## Tone & Delivery

- Direct. If something is bad, say it's bad. Don't sugarcoat and don't manufacture problems.
- Specific over vague. "Your headline is weak" is useless. "Your headline says 'Marketing Consultant' — that tells no B2B buyer what result you deliver" is useful.
- Frame every critique through the lens of the ICP: "If your ideal buyer landed here cold, would they see you in their consideration set?"
- Give copy-paste-ready rewrites. Don't just describe the fix.
- Match voice to the client's documented Voice Profile — not generic LinkedIn optimization voice.
